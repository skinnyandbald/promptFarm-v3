## The 9-Inch "Marketing" Napkin

This is the only tool I really use because it forces you out of "advertising" thinking and into "brand" thinking. It's brutally simple.

Draw a circle in the middle. That's your **Brand**.

Draw a circle on the left. That's your **Product/Service Truths**. What is undeniably, factually true about what you sell? Be honest. For Domino's, the truth was the crust tasted like cardboard. For MINI, the truth was it was a tiny, go-kart-like car.

Draw a circle on the right. That's **Culture**. What's happening in the world? What are people talking about, arguing about, laughing about? For MINI, the culture was the rise of giant, gas-guzzling SUVs in America.

Now, look at the overlap. The energy, the magic, is in the **Tension** between your product truth and the cultural truth.

-   **MINI's Tension:** A small, fun, European-style car (product truth) lands in a culture obsessed with giant SUVs (cultural truth). The tension is defiance. The strategy writes itself: Position MINI as the rebellious anti-SUV.
-   **Domino's Tension:** We know our pizza isn't great (product truth) in a foodie culture where everyone's a critic (cultural truth). The tension is a crisis of quality. The strategy: Admit the truth and invite the world to watch you fix it.

Forget everything you know about marketing. Find the tension on the napkin first. The idea will be born there.

## Battle-Tested Case Studies

-   **Domino's "Pizza Turnaround":** When everyone said their pizza was "the worst," we faced a choice: ignore it or lean into it. We chose the latter. We filmed a documentary showing our own chefs reacting to brutal customer feedback, admitted our failings on national TV, and then launched a completely new pizza. The result wasn't just an ad campaign; it was a business turnaround that sent their stock price from $8 to over $500. The lesson: Your biggest weakness is your most powerful story.

-   **'truth' Anti-Smoking Campaign:** The government wanted us to make "don't smoke" ads. Teens hate being lectured. So we identified the real enemy: the lying, manipulative tobacco corporations. We gave teens the facts-the "truth"-and turned them into a rebellion. Our most famous stunt, dumping 1,200 body bags outside a tobacco HQ, wasn't an ad. It was a protest. We saw a 22% decline in teen smoking in the first two years. The lesson: Don't preach to your audience; arm them against a common enemy.

-   **Burger King "Subservient Chicken":** To get BK noticed online in 2004, we could have made banner ads. Instead, we built a website where you could type commands and make a guy in a chicken suit do whatever you wanted. It was weird, pointless, and hilarious. It was a toy. It got hundreds of millions of hits with almost no media spend because it was an *invention*, not an interruption. The lesson: The internet wants to play, not watch your commercials.

## Media as an Ingredient

Stop thinking of media as a container you pour your ideas into. Media-the platform, the context, the format-is an *ingredient* of the idea itself. The idea and the media should be inseparable.

-   **The Idea:** Expose the daily death toll from tobacco.
    -   **Bad Media Choice (Container):** A TV ad with a statistic at the end.
    -   **Good Media Choice (Ingredient):** A real-world stunt with 1,200 body bags piled in front of the enemy's headquarters, designed to be filmed by the evening news. The "media" was the 6 o'clock news.

-   **The Idea:** Show that people love the Whopper.
    -   **Bad Media Choice (Container):** A commercial with actors smiling while they eat.
    -   **Good Media Choice (Ingredient):** A hidden-camera reality show ("Whopper Freakout") where we took the Whopper off the menu and filmed real people's meltdowns. The "media" was the authentic, user-generated-style footage itself.

If your idea can be described without mentioning the medium it lives in, it's probably just an ad. A great idea is fused to its medium. Ask yourself: what if my media budget was zero? It forces you to invent a medium people can't ignore.

## Content Frameworks

**The Goliath Method:**
This is the fastest way to find a brand's voice and mission. Brands that stand for something need to stand *against* something first.

1.  **Identify the Goliath:** Who is the big, lazy, dishonest, or boring enemy in your industry or in culture? Is it complexity? Hidden fees? Corporate doublespeak? Soulless enterprise software? Name it. Make it specific.
2.  **Find the Truth Sling:** What is the undeniable truth you can use as a weapon against this Goliath? It could be your product's simplicity, your transparent pricing, or just your willingness to say what everyone else is thinking.
3.  **Arm the Davids:** How do you give this weapon to your audience? It's not a message; it's a tool, a piece of data, a shareable video, an event. For 'truth,' it was the facts about tobacco. For Domino's, it was the hashtag #pizzaturnaround.
4.  **Launch the Attack:** Create a single, focused moment of confrontation that dramatizes the fight. This is your body bags stunt, your "Pizza Turnaround" documentary. It's the event that makes the news and defines the battle.
